Frequently Asked Questions

Below are answers to the most common questions about the 32-team playoff proposal. Click any question to expand the answer.

General Questions

Q: Isn't 32 teams too many?

No, because every lower NCAA division followed similar evolutionary paths, expanding their playoffs as membership grew and conferences multiplied.

Q: Would the 32-team playoff devalue the regular season?

The opposite is true. The regular season can determine:

  • Conference championships: Winners get automatic bids
  • At-large selection: 22 competitive spots based on committee rankings
  • Seeding (1-16): Determines who gets home-field advantage
  • Top 8 vs. Top 16: Difference between two home games and one
  • Seeded vs. Unseeded: Massive difference between seed 16 (home game) and being the top unseeded team (road game)

Q: How does this compare to other NCAA divisions?

FBS currently has the worst playoff access in NCAA football:

  • FCS (128 teams): 24-team playoff = 18.75% access
  • Division II (160 teams): 32-team playoff = 20.0% access
  • Division III (245 teams): 40-team playoff = 16.32% access
  • FBS (138 teams): 12-team playoff = 8.7% access

Division II just expanded to 32 teams in 2025 with 16 conferences. FBS has 138 teams and 10 conferences. The math works even better. See full historical analysis →

Q: What about the SEC and Big Ten disagreement?

The current impasse proves why partial solutions fail. The SEC wants 16 teams, the Big Ten wants 24 teams. Neither solves the fundamental problems.

The 32-team format gives both what they need:

  • SEC gets depth: 22 at-large bids means they can send 6-8 teams in strong years
  • Big Ten gets access: Same broad opportunities without needing guaranteed multiple autobids and majority at-large bids; they also get depth like the SEC
  • All 10 conferences get guaranteed autobids
  • No one needs to break away

Q: Why not 8, 12, 16, or 24 teams instead of 32?

Each alternative format has been proposed or implemented, and each creates significant problems that 32 teams solves:

8 teams:

  • History proves it's unstable—FCS tried it for exactly ONE season (1981) before expanding
  • Too exclusive with 10 FBS conferences; multiple conference champions would be excluded
  • Would provide only 5.8% playoff access (8 out of 138 teams), even worse than the current 8.7%
  • No NCAA division has found 8 teams sustainable long-term

12 teams (current format):

  • Conference championships become redundant when both participants are playoff-bound (2024 and 2025 saw both the SEC and Big Ten Champion and runner-up in the playoff field regardless)
  • Bye teams struggle with long layoffs (1-7 record in quarterfinals)
  • Teams 13-16 have legitimate exclusion grievances
  • Only 5 conferences get autobids out of 10—arbitrary selection
  • Access is shrinking as FBS grows (9.0% in 2024 → 8.8% in 2025 → 8.7% in 2026)

16 teams:

  • Impossible math with 10 conferences
  • Big Ten's (and SEC's Initial) Proposal: Multiple automatic bids for power conferences, but still only leaving one automatic bid for the non-power FBS conferences
  • SEC's proposal: 5 automatic bids (however, only one automatic bid is for the non-power FBS conferences) + 11 at-large (which non-power FBS conference feasibly gets the automatic bid? Creates arbitrary selection)
  • If all 10 conferences got autobids: only 6 at-large spots remaining, which is insufficient for power conference depth
  • Either excludes conferences (two-tier system) or squeezes at-large opportunities (insufficient merit-based selection)

24 teams:

  • Rest discrepancies would still happen, but this time between the top 8 seeds and any team playing in the first round
  • The gap between seed 8 (bye) and seed 9 (no bye) becomes the new controversy
  • Big Ten's initial proposal of 4 autobids per power conference and only two for non-power FBS conferences and their latest current proposal of the 23+1 format both undermine the concept of "conference champion" and removes meritocracy

Why 32 teams solves all these problems:

  • 10 autobids for all conference champions (access and equity)
  • 22 at-large bids (merit-based selection, sufficient for power conference depth)
  • No byes: everyone plays the same number of postseason games (competitive balance)
  • Top 16 teams seeded with home-field advantage (rewards regular season)
  • 23.2% playoff access (though more generous than Division I FCS, Division II, and Division III, it is still in line with NCAA Division I norms of 18-28%)
  • Follows proven precedent:
    • Division II (160 teams) just expanded to 32 in 2025
    • Division III (245 teams) has done 32 teams from 2005-23 until expanding to 40 teams to make room for at-large bids

For more information, you can refer to the detailed analysis of why each format fails.

Q: Isn't this too many games for student-athletes?

The national champion and runner-up would play no more than 17-19 games total (12-14 regular season games + 5 playoff games). For comparison:

  • FCS champions and runner-ups play 16 or 17 games (12 regular + 4 or 5 playoff games) (except for the Ivy League, where if any team makes it to the FCS championship game, they would play up to 14 or 15 games (10 regular season games + 4 or 5 playoff games))
  • Division II champions and runner-ups play 15 or 16 games (10 or 11 regular season games + 5 playoff games)
  • Division III champions and runner-ups play 15 or 16 games (10 regular season games + 5 or 6 playoff games) (except for the NESCAC, where if any team makes it to the Division III championship game, they would play up to 14 or 15 games (9 regular season games + 5 or 6 playoff games))
  • NFL teams play up to 21 games (17 regular season games + up to 4 playoff games)

The NCAA already has protocols for managing player workload and injury risk across multiple divisions. This is a solved problem, not a new challenge.

Proposed Process Questions

Q: What about conferences that want to keep championship games?

My proposal recommends eliminating conference championship games to avoid scheduling conflicts and bye week issues. However, conferences that insist on keeping them (like the SEC) can do so. The winner would still receive the automatic bid to the FBS playoff. However, for conferences that do want to hold conference championship games, the teams that do not make it will play one less game than teams in conferences that only determine the champion concluding the regular season.

The optimal solution remains determining conference champions through regular season records with tiebreakers, as this eliminates these complications.

Q: Do all Division I FBS teams have to play a 14-game schedule?

No. The purpose of providing 12-game, 13-game, and 14-game schedule options is to provide flexibility for all of Division I FBS. Some conferences can still opt for a 12-game schedule if they so choose, while other conferences (such as the power conferences) could elect to expand their regular season schedule up to 14 games to make up for eliminating the conference championship games. Regardless of the number of regular season games played, every team will end the regular season on the same weekend except Army and Navy. Every team except Army and Navy would end the regular season on the weekend before Thanksgiving, while Army and Navy would conclude the regular season on Saturday after Thanksgiving with the selection show to follow afterward.

Lower NCAA Divisions already play a different number of regular season games. In Division I FCS, while nearly all teams play a 12-game regular season schedule since 2026, teams in the Ivy League continued playing a 10-game schedule containing 7 conference games and 3 non-conference games every year. In Division II, some play a 10-game regular season schedule while others play an 11-game regular season schedule with all teams playing a various number of non-conference and conference games. In Division III, nearly all teams play a 10-game regular season schedule with varying number of non-conference and conference games. Teams in the NESCAC play a 9-game conference-only schedule every year. You can learn more about this in lower NCAA Division season structures.

Once again, the optimal solution remains determining conference champions through regular season records with tiebreakers, as this eliminates these complications.

Q: Won't this kill the bowl system?

No, the bowl system is enhanced, not eliminated due to the following:

  • The New Year's Six bowls (Rose, Sugar, Orange, Cotton, Peach, Fiesta) host quarterfinals and semifinals on a rotating basis
  • The top four Non-New Year's Six Bowl Games (Citrus, Alamo, ReliaQuest, Holiday) would select teams eliminated in the second round of the FBS Playoff.
  • An option to implement Wildcard Bowl Games for teams eliminated in the first round of the FBS Playoff is provided.
  • Secondary playoff system provides additional postseason opportunities. A total of 22 additional bowl games would be in the secondary playoff system.
  • Traditional bowls continue for teams that neither make the FBS playoffs nor secondary playoff system.

This is similar to college basketball, where the NCAA Tournament coexists with the NIT and other postseason tournaments.

Q: How long would the playoff last?

The playoff would last from the first weekend of December through the second Friday/Saturday of January:

  • First Round: First weekend of December (Wed-Sat)
  • Second Round: Second week of December (Wed-Sat)
  • Quarterfinals: Third weekend of December (Fri-Sat)
  • Semifinals: January 1 (or January 2 if New Year's falls on Sunday)
  • National Championship: Second Friday or Saturday of January

The national champion and runner-up would play 17-19 total games (12-14 regular season games + 5 playoff games), comparable to FCS champions and within established NCAA and NFL norms.

Q: Would you be open to doing playoff games of the first two rounds on Thursday instead of Wednesday?

I am open to the possibility. However, the NFL dominates the Thursday Night landscape as well as Monday Night during the regular season. In an ideal scenario, the NFL would permanently eliminate Thursday Night Football. The only way NFL Thursday Night Football would be permanently eliminated is if the NFL makes an official announcement that they will eliminate Thursday Night Football. However, I strongly doubt the NFL would eliminate Thursday Night Football.

In the additional adjustments page, I did mention that the first round would happen on Black Friday and Saturday after Thanksgiving as well as the second round happening from Thursday-Saturday if the FBS playoffs would begin on Thanksgiving weekend. However, we would be competing with the NFL for viewership if we do go this route. This is also the reason why I found starting the FBS playoff on the first weekend of December is ideal. It gives the regular season enough room to have the Army-Navy game as the stand-alone regular season finale.

Q: How about quarterfinal games at campus sites? Why not have one quarterfinal game on Friday and three quarterfinal games on Saturday?

Having quarterfinal games on campus sites will likely involve tweaking the bowl line up. While the New Year's Six Bowl Games would still be safe, two of the four proposed second round consolation games would likely be downgraded as hosts for the Secondary Playoff System. Let's say the Holiday Bowl and ReliaQuest Bowls are moved as hosts for the secondary playoff system. This time, the Rose Bowl would become a rotating semifinal game along with the five remaining New Year's Six Bowl Games, the Citrus Bowl and Alamo Bowl. These eight bowl games would likely be in a rotation where they would take the second round playoff participants and quarterfinalists as well as being rotating semifinal hosts in a 4-year cycle. The media networks for the final two rounds will likely become different as well. ESPN/ABC/ESPN DTC, CBS/Paramount+, FOX/FOX One, and NBC/Peacock would rotate to air all of the final two rounds instead of rotating to air the other semifinal game and National Championship Game.

In addition, we also have to deal with the NFL as well. The NFL begins their Saturday late regular season games on the third weekend of December. This would mean that if we want to have three quarterfinal games on Saturday, we would be competing with the NFL on that third Saturday of December. In addition, there are also major college basketball games happening during that weekend as well (such as the CBS Sports Classic). The only option is to have three quarterfinal games on Friday and one on Saturday to avoid overlap with the NFL as well as College Basketball.

Q: Why not expand beyond 32 teams?

While larger fields are theoretically possible, 32 teams is the sweet spot because of the following:

  • Fits within existing schedule constraints (Army-Navy stays after Thanksgiving, Rose Bowl stays New Year's Day)
  • Healthy autobid-to-at-large ratio (31% autobids maintains conference championship importance)
  • 23.2% playoff access (comparable to NCAA Division I norms of 18-28%)
  • Five-round bracket structure is mathematically clean and traditional
  • Solves FBS's actual problem (access + depth) without creating new problems (excessive at-large bids)

Historical Archives Questions

Q: In your season archives, 1998 was the oldest season you went back to. Why did you not go past 1998 and start at 1978 (FBS's first season) instead?

I could theoretically extend back to 1978, but data quality becomes spotty and justifying selections gets harder. The 1998 start captures the modern era comprehensively with 28 seasons of verifiable data.

1978-85 was a very weird transition as a few of the conferences were hybrids of teams in Division I FBS (formerly I-A) and Division I FCS (I-AA). Those seasons would have likely been very tricky for me to determine what a 32-team field would have looked like had they happen. In addition, as former Penn State head coach Joe Paterno advocated for a playoff system back in January 1978, bowl organizers back then were not very open to a college football playoff. However, as the controversy grew in the 1990s, where not all seasons were able to pit the top two teams into the same bowl game, the bowl organizers were left with no choice but to accept a system that would truly pit the top two teams to play for a National Championship. The BCS system was really the first true system to put the top two teams to play for a national championship, which is why 1998 was the best season for me to start.

Q: If you were to extend back to 1978, which teams would have benefitted?

There are a few notable teams that would benefit if I did extend back to 1978. Nebraska would have definitely benefitted where they would have shown their dominance by making appearances in the 32-team field in all of the seasons from 1978-1997 under Tom Osborne. Ohio State would also have benefitted as well. They would have playoff appearances in the 32-team field at least from 1979-1986 as well as 1990-97. They would be on the fringe in 1978 and 1989 (but would not have made it in 1987 and 1988).

In addition, New Mexico State, Eastern Michigan, and Wyoming are a few other notable teams that would have likely benefitted as well. New Mexico State would have made their only apperance in 1978 as a conference champion (and would have the longest drought of any FBS team with 47 seasons since their last appearance). Eastern Michigan would have made their only appearance in 1987 as a MAC Champion (but would also have one of the longest droughts of any FBS team with 38 seasons since their last appearance), and Wyoming would be an at-large contender for the 32-team field in 1996.

However, as I mentioned in a previous FAQ, data quality becomes spotty and justifying selections gets harder. The 1998 start captures the modern era comprehensively with 28 seasons of verifiable data.
