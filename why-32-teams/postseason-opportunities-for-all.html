Postseason Opportunities for All

High School Football is Vast

Consider the bigger picture other than the best FBS teams competing for a national championship. Many high schools field football teams, whether the football teams are varsity, junior varsity, C teams, or freshman-exclusive teams. About 1,029,588 high school students play tackle football, which is significantly higher than the number of players on any collegiate football teams, whether Division I FBS or FCS, Division II, Division III, or NAIA. In addition, high school football teams do not have a top limit regarding the number of players on a roster. However, some local districts can set a limit regarding roster sizes.

College Football Rosters

Not many high school players would go on to play College Football. With the new House Settlement on June 6, 2025, Division I FBS and Division I FCS have roster limits of 105 players. Division II and Division III football have no roster limits. However, let's pretend that Division II and Division III teams can field 100 players. While unrelated to the NCAA, NAIA has an average roster size of 104 football players, while NJCAA has a roster limit of 85 and CCCAA has a roster limit of 55. Here is a breakdown of the total players that play in any meaningful college football at any level (numbers accurate as of the most recent completed season):

Chances of Making the NFL is Very Small

About 8.4% of high school football players would go on to continue to play football at the collegiate level, whether he plays on full scholarship (Division I FBS and FCS), partial scholarship (Division I FBS, Division I FCS, Division II and NAIA), or walks on (playing without a scholarship) with the team. The NCAA limits FBS football to 105 full scholarships per team (which can be split as partial scholarships) and limits FCS football to 63 full scholarships per team (which can also be split as partial scholarships). In addition, some FCS teams have lower scholarship limits than other FCS teams:

  • In the Patriot League, teams only offered 15 equivalent full scholarships beginning in 2013, with the potential to add 15 per season. Teams in the Patriot League are allowed to have 60 equivalent full scholarships limit per team.
  • The Northeast Conference used to only allow 45 scholarships, but now will allow as many as 105 scholarships. However, most schools are unlikely to offer more than 50 scholarships.
  • The Pioneer Football League and the Ivy League do not offer scholarships to football players.

Division II contains 161 football teams, and only offers partial scholarships for football players, which has the equivalent value of 36 full scholarships per team. Division III contains 245 football teams, but do not offer scholarships to any football player. While not related to the NCAA, the NAIA has 94 football teams but can only offer up to the equivalent value of 24 full scholarships per team and can split those scholarships as partial scholarships. The California Community College Athletic Association (CCCAA) do not offer scholarships, while Division I NJCAA teams do offer scholarships, but Division III NJCAA teams do not.

The NFL Draft currently has 255 selections in seven rounds (unless teams are forced to forfeit their draft picks due to violating the NFL rules). Out of all the players who continued their football career in college, NFL teams only select about 0.3% of all collegiate players in the NFL Draft. The majority of the selections come from players playing in the Division I FBS level. Looking at the high school football perspective, NFL teams would draft only 0.025% of football players who have played high school and collegiate football (regardless of playing with or without a scholarship). On the other side of declaring for the NFL Draft, not many college football players can sustain a long NFL career, especially whether it depends on the position played and how many good off-the-field choices they make.

Most Cannot Play Football Forever

At some point, accumulating injuries and diminished passion would get the best of them and would force them to retire from playing football and pursue career opportunities after football. However, a football career could also gradually or even immediately be over with continued wrong off-the-field decisions. Some even retire from their football careers during college, and most high school players retire from their football careers before attending college.

32-Team Playoff Gives Moments Student Athletes May Not Get in the NFL

NFL scouts would love to observe and evaluate college prospects in their postseason performance during the extended postseason, even in intense environments such as crowd noise and harsh winter-like elements. That is because the NFL playoffs does happen during January, where some teams have to play in cold-weather environments, culminating in the Super Bowl on the Second Sunday of February. The 32-team playoff system would allow it because of the following reasons:

  • The circumstances that football players could either retire after their collegiate career is over or during their collegiate career.
  • Play for the love of postseason football with a March Madness feel.
  • Players who want to play in the NFL can prove that they can play in harsh elements and environments during the postseason and late in the regular season if it happens.

In addition, unlike the NFL where roster spots are extremely limited, more student athletes would be able to experience a postseason environment in the 32-team field.

  • With 32 teams making the FBS Playoffs, with the 105 player limit, about 3,360 Division I FBS athletes that are part of the 32-team field would play for a National Championship every year.
  • Adding in the 32-team College Playoff Crown as the secondary playoff system, about 6,720 Division I FBS athletes would have a means to participate in the postseason every year.
  • Adding in the 7 remaining stand-alone bowl games, that would go to about 8,190 Division I FBS athletes having a means to participate in the postseason every year.

If you are still concerned about exclusivity, it still exists.

  • Only about 105 Division I FBS athletes (on one team) out of the 8,190 Division I FBS athletes participating in the postseason would win a National Championship every year. That is only about 1.3% of Division I FBS athletes playing in the postseason that would help their team win a National Championship every year.
  • If we want to go deeper, only about 105 Division I FBS athletes (on one team) out of the 14,490 Division I FBS athletes would win a National Championship every year. That is only about 0.7% of Division I FBS athletes that would help their team win a National Championship every year.

Whether a team makes it into the 32-team field, the CPC, or a bowl game, this is much more than players that make the postseason in the NFL. Only 742 players out of 1696 players in the NFL would participate in the NFL playoffs. However, only 53 players (on one team) would go on to win the Super Bowl every year. By adding in the 32-team FBS Playoff, the 32-Team CPC and the 7 remaining stand-alone bowl games, Division I FBS would still provide the balance to give more student athletes opportunities to play in the postseason, while still providing the exclusivity of winning a National Championship.
