The Crisis Hovering in College Football

The SEC and Big Ten are at a crossroads. After not agreeing to a format, the playoff stayed at 12 teams for at least another season. However, the impasse goes beyond college football. Despite forming an alliance in February 2024, we saw that it fractured quickly as the SEC and Big Ten went in different directions. How did we get to this point? Let's start with the recent conference alignment that plagued college football.

Conference Realignment of 2020s

SEC Expands with Oklahoma and Texas

The new conference realignment paved the comeback of super conferences. Texas and Oklahoma, the Big 12’s two biggest athletic brands, were looking to join a new conference. Both wanted to leave the Big 12 in order to expand their brand. They reached out to the Southeastern Conference to become new members of the conference. On July 29, 2021, the SEC members unanimously approve to invite Texas and Oklahoma to join the SEC. Both approved their invitations the next day, expanding the SEC from 14 teams to 16 teams beginning on July 1, 2025, making a return to Super Conferences. However, Texas, Oklahoma, and the Big 12 eventually agreed to let the Red River Rivals join the SEC beginning on July 1, 2024.

The eight remaining Big 12 teams stood pat and looked into the best teams from the non-Power FBS conferences as expansion candidates. About six weeks after Texas and Oklahoma announced their intentions to join the SEC, the eight remaining Big 12 teams unanimously approved extending invitations to FBS Independent BYU and AAC members Cincinnati, Houston, and UCF. BYU, Cincinnati, Houston, and UCF all accepted to join the Big 12. On June 15, 2022, they agreed to move there beginning the 2023 FBS season.

The Pac 12's Collapse and Rebuild

About 11 months after Texas and Oklahoma made their move to the SEC, the Big Ten received a gift from two Los Angeles universities. On June 30, 2022, USC and UCLA officially decided to leave the Pac-12 to join the Big Ten beginning with the 2024-25 academic year. Big Ten members unanimously approved inviting USC and UCLA. The move to the Big Ten increased the Big Ten membership to 16 teams. The Big Ten now have schools that go across the country. USC and UCLA’s move stunned the college athletic landscape as the Pac-12 loses two of their biggest brands to the Big Ten. The UC Board of Reagents eventually approved UCLA’s move to the Big Ten on December 14, 2022.

Conference realignment continued in Summer 2023. The Pac-12 was truly gutted in 6 weeks. On July 27, 2023, the Big 12 unanimously welcomed back Colorado, which went into effect in August 2024. The Pac-12 attempted to keep their remaining members together. However, one week later, their effort failed as five more members departed to different conferences. On August 4, 2023, the Big Ten greeted Oregon and Washington to join their conference starting in August 2024. The Big Ten became the largest Power Conference at the time with 18 members. The Big 12 acclimated Arizona, Arizona State, and Utah, which now increased to 16 members beginning in July 2024. On September 1, 2023, Stanford and California became members of the ACC beginning in August 2024. SMU also became an ACC member as well beginning in July 2024. The ACC increased their membership to 17 full football members. Counting Notre Dame’s membership as a full non-football member, they tie the Big Ten with 18 members overall.

After many of its members left for different conferences, the Pac-12 rebuilt their brand. Oregon State and Washington State reached a settlement with the ten departing Pac-12 members for control of what’s left of the Pac-12 and rebuild the conference. On December 1, 2023, Oregon State and Washington State formed a temporary alliance with the Mountain West, where each would play 6 Mountain West teams and five Power conference teams plus one FCS team, but only for 2024. Even though the extension of the agreement fell through, Oregon State and Washington State are still both committed to rebuilding the Pac-12. On September 12, 2024, Boise State, Colorado State, Fresno State, and San Diego State will join the Pac-12 beginning in July 2026. Utah State pledged to join the Pac-12 12 days later. Although they have added Gonzaga as a full non-football member, they still needed at least one more full football member to join the conference. On June 30, 2025, Texas State officially made their decision to leave the Sun Belt for the Pac-12. The Pac-12 completed their rebuild and survived. However, they could still be looking to add more members in the future.

Additional Conference Responses

The AAC was able to backfill after losing Cincinnati, Houston, and UCF. On October 21, 2021, the AAC invited UAB, North Texas, Texas-San Antonio (UTSA), Florida Atlantic, Charlotte, and Rice from CUSA, where all six of them accepted to join. The AAC had an initial different plan. They initially wanted to plan a merger with Stanford, California, Oregon State, and Washington State. However, after Stanford, California, and SMU decided to join the ACC, the AAC decided to no longer pursue the merger. Instead, on October 25, 2023, Army decided to end their FBS Independent status and joined the AAC as a football affiliate to replace SMU. The Army-Navy game still remains as an annual non-conference game. A year after Army joined, the AAC rebranded once again as American Conference since July 2025.

The Mountain West responded to the Pac-12 developments. After losing five full members to the Pac-12, the six remaining full members (UNLV, Wyoming, Air Force, Nevada, San Jose State, and New Mexico) committed to stay with the conference. They were able to elevate Hawaii from football only affiliate member to full-time member and recruited UTEP to join their conference, giving them enough members to operate as an FBS conference. Northern Illinois will join as a Football Affiliate beginning 2026 (and putting their other sports to the Horizon League), further stabilizing the conference for now. In addition, 10-time FCS Champion North Dakota State will also join as a Football Affiliate beginning in 2026 (while also keeping all their other sports in the Summit League). Although they were able to also add Grand Canyon and UC-Davis as full non-football members, the Mountain West might not be done with expanding.

CUSA fought through survivability during this round of realignment. CUSA lost nine members to two different conferences throughout October 2021. They lost six to the AAC and three more to the Sun Belt. CUSA was able to subsist despite the massive exodus. On November 5, 2021, two FBS independent members, New Mexico State and Liberty, and two FCS universities, Jacksonville State and Sam Houston State, accepted to become full members of CUSA. All of these moves took effect on July 1, 2023. On October 14, 2022, CUSA scored a major move by adding Kennesaw State (who is jumping from FCS to FBS) as a full member of the conference, pushing their membership to 10 teams for 2024. A little over a year later, on November 28, 2023, Delaware jumped to the FBS level to join CUSA, pushing their membership up to 11 teams. On May 10, 2024, Missouri State also made the jump to the FBS level to join CUSA, pushing their membership to 12 teams starting in 2025. However, they will lose two members to different conferences. UTEP will leave for the Mountain West in 2026, while Louisiana Tech will leave to re-join the Sun Belt no later than 2027.

The Sun Belt became a beneficiary in conference realignment during the 2020s. Throughout the rest of October 2021, Southern Miss, Old Dominion, and Marshall all announced their intentions to leave CUSA for the Sun Belt, and James Madison from the FCS ranks joined in as well. They were able to balloon up to 14 members. Although they lost Texas State to the Pac-12, they were able to respond by accepting Louisiana Tech as a full football member.

The MAC had been relatively quiet for most of the conference realignment until recently. UMass initially was a MAC-football only affiliate for four seasons and still had all their sports in the Atlantic 10 when they first moved up to the FBS back in 2011. From 2016-24, UMass football played as an FBS Independent, but had little to no success. On February 29, 2024, UMass rejoins the MAC as a full member, which goes into effect beginning July 1, 2025. With the MAC going away from Divisions, having an odd number of teams would be no problem for them. However, that would only last for the 2025 season, as Northern Illinois becomes a football only affiliate for the Mountain West beginning in 2026.

CFP Reworks Agreement

The College Football Playoff underwent its most significant transformation since its inception in 2014. On December 1, 2022, the CFP Board of Managers unanimously approved expanding from a 4-team playoff to a 12-team format beginning with the 2024 season. The decision came after years of debate about access and competitive balance, with non-Power conference teams rarely receiving invitations to the 4-team playoff despite undefeated seasons.

However, the playoff expansion came with a significant restructuring of CFP governance that fundamentally altered the balance of power in college football. In March 2024, the CFP signed a six-year, $7.8 billion media rights contract with ESPN extending through the 2031-32 season. During those contract negotiations, the 10 FBS commissioners and Notre Dame athletic director Pete Bevacqua signed a memorandum of understanding that granted the Big Ten and SEC "the bulk of control over the playoff's future format," according to multiple reports.

This memorandum of understanding represented a dramatic shift from the previous governance structure where all Power Conferences held equal voting power. SEC Commissioner Greg Sankey acknowledged the new power arrangement publicly at SEC Media Days in July 2025, stating that the document "very clearly grants" decision-making authority to the combination of the SEC and Big Ten, adding that "ultimately we have to use that authority with great wisdom and discretion." Under the new structure, changes to the future playoff format no longer require approval from the CFP's board of managers, which is composed of university presidents and chancellors. Instead, the commissioners and Notre Dame's athletic director make these decisions, with the SEC and Big Ten effectively holding veto power.

This governance restructuring reflected brutal economic reality. The SEC and Big Ten controlled the majority of college football's television viewership, recruiting talent, and revenue generation. FOX Sports estimated that the new CFP deal would generate $20-22 million per school annually, with the SEC and Big Ten demanding 58% of playoff revenue starting in 2026. Television networks were primarily paying for SEC and Big Ten content, with other conferences providing supplementary programming. During spring 2024 CFP negotiations, the two mega-conferences threatened to break away and create their own playoff if they didn't receive majority control and revenue share.

The other eight FBS conferences and Notre Dame acquiesced to these demands, essentially "handing over control," as multiple sources described it. The memorandum of understanding only covers a 12- or 14-team playoff format, meaning any expansion to 16, 24, or more teams would require both the SEC and Big Ten to agree on new terms. This created a situation where both mega-conferences needed to consent for any significant changes to proceed.

The timing of this power shift proved catastrophic for reaching consensus on future expansion. By requiring both the SEC and Big Ten to agree, the CFP contract made it nearly impossible to implement any playoff format they didn't both enthusiastically support. And as would become clear through 2024 and 2025, the two conferences had very different ideas about what the playoff should look like, even as they agreed that 12 teams wasn't enough. The governance structure that was supposed to prevent the SEC and Big Ten from being outvoted by smaller conferences instead created a situation where the two mega-conferences could deadlock each other, bringing playoff expansion to a complete standstill.

SEC-Big Ten Alliance

On February 9, 2024, the SEC and Big Ten announced a groundbreaking alliance that would reshape college athletics. The joint announcement from SEC Commissioner Greg Sankey and Big Ten Commissioner Tony Petitti described an "historic partnership" focused on "student-athlete welfare," "competitive excellence," and "the future of college sports." The two conferences, representing 34 of the most powerful athletic programs in the nation, committed to working together on scheduling, playoff format discussions, and media rights negotiations.

The stated goals of the alliance sounded noble. Both conferences emphasized their commitment to maintaining academic standards for student-athletes, ensuring appropriate rest periods between games, and preserving the traditions that made college football special. They spoke about creating opportunities for intersectional play, with potential regular-season games between SEC and Big Ten opponents becoming marquee events. They promised to collaborate on playoff expansion discussions to ensure a fair and competitive postseason format.

The reality was far more calculated. The SEC-Big Ten Alliance was fundamentally about power consolidation and revenue maximization. With 34 schools between them, the two conferences controlled nearly 30% of all FBS programs but commanded far more than 30% of television viewership, recruiting rankings, and athletic department revenues. By joining forces, they could effectively dictate terms to the CFP, media partners, bowl games, and other conferences. They didn't need to break away from the NCAA or FBS structure if they could simply control it from within.

The alliance created immediate strategic advantages. In media rights negotiations, the two conferences could present a unified front, ensuring maximum payouts from networks desperate for their content. In playoff discussions, they could jointly advocate for formats that guaranteed multiple bids for each conference while limiting access for smaller conferences. In scheduling, they could coordinate to avoid cannibalizing each other's strength of schedule arguments while scheduling enough intersectional games to boost both conferences' overall profiles.

However, cracks in the alliance appeared almost immediately. The SEC and Big Ten might share common interests in dominating college football, but they remained fierce competitors for recruits, coaches, television ratings, and playoff spots. Every SEC team in the playoff meant one fewer Big Ten team, and vice versa. Every five-star recruit who chose Alabama was a recruit who didn't choose Ohio State. The conferences might cooperate when facing external challenges, but they would compete ruthlessly when dividing the spoils.

The alliance also faced philosophical differences that proved difficult to bridge. The Big Ten prided itself on academic standards and research university partnerships, with many members belonging to the prestigious Association of American Universities. The SEC embraced a football-first mentality where winning championships took priority over almost everything else. The Big Ten valued traditional bowl affiliations, particularly the Rose Bowl's tie to the conference champion. The SEC was more willing to abandon tradition if it meant more money or playoff access.

Geographic and cultural differences added to the tension. Big Ten schools stretched from Rutgers in New Jersey to USC in Los Angeles, creating travel nightmares and time zone complications. The SEC remained more regionally concentrated, which simplified logistics but potentially limited their national media appeal. Big Ten athletic directors worried about student-athletes missing excessive class time for cross-country travel. SEC athletic directors worried their teams might be disadvantaged in recruiting against Big Ten schools offering closer-to-home opportunities.

By mid-2024, the alliance had become more symbolic than substantive. The conferences continued to coordinate on certain issues, particularly when negotiating with the CFP or NCAA, but the promised regular-season scheduling agreements never materialized in meaningful numbers. Joint initiatives on student-athlete welfare produced few concrete changes. Most tellingly, the two conferences began proposing different solutions to the playoff expansion impasse, each prioritizing their own interests over alliance unity.

The SEC-Big Ten Alliance ultimately represented a marriage of convenience rather than a genuine partnership. Both conferences recognized they were stronger together than apart when facing external pressures, but neither was willing to subordinate its own goals to alliance harmony. The alliance would hold as long as it served both parties' interests, but would fracture the moment those interests diverged significantly. And when it came to playoff expansion, those interests diverged dramatically.

SEC and Big Ten Propose Different Solutions

As playoff expansion negotiations dragged through 2024 and into 2025, the fundamental incompatibility between SEC and Big Ten interests became impossible to ignore. Both conferences wanted more playoff spots for their teams, but they disagreed sharply on how to structure a system that would maximize their respective advantages. What emerged was a complex dance of competing proposals that shifted dramatically over time, exposing deep philosophical rifts between the two mega-conferences.

The Initial Push: 14-Team Playoff with 3-3-2-2-1 Format

The first serious expansion proposal surfaced in February 2024, when CFP officials began discussing a 14-team format. According to multiple reports, the SEC and Big Ten initially proposed a model giving each conference four automatic bids, which would have left only one at-large spot in a 14-team field. This "egregious" proposal, as one college administrator described it, faced immediate pushback from the ACC, Big 12, and other conferences.

The proposal quickly evolved into what became known as the "3-3-2-2-1" format: three automatic bids each for the SEC and Big Ten, two each for the ACC and Big 12, one for the highest-ranked Group of Five champion, and three at-large selections. Ross Dellenger of Yahoo Sports reported that this format was "being socialized among major conference administrators" as a compromise to the original four-bid proposal. However, even this scaled-back version faced skepticism from conferences that would receive only two guaranteed spots while the SEC and Big Ten secured three each.

The Big Ten's Evolution: The 4-4-2-2-1 Model and Beyond

By late 2024 and early 2025, the discussion shifted to a 16-team playoff, with the Big Ten leading the charge for what became known as the "4-4-2-2-1-3" format. This proposal would grant four automatic berths each to the Big Ten and SEC, two each to the ACC and Big 12, one to the highest-ranked Group of Five conference champion, and three at-large bids (with potential protection for Notre Dame). Big Ten Commissioner Tony Petitti championed this approach at Big Ten media days in July 2025, emphasizing "a playoff system that allocates spots based on conference standings and the results of playing games."

The Big Ten's rationale centered on reducing reliance on what they perceived as an inconsistent selection committee. "Make it about how you compete against your conference and take out any sort of perceived bias or politicking and campaigning," one Big Ten source explained. The format would also enable season-ending conference championship play-in games, where the Big Ten could pit their third, fourth, fifth, and sixth-placed teams against each other in on-campus matchups during conference championship weekend, creating additional high-stakes inventory for television partners.

The Big Ten later evolved this thinking even further. By November 2025, Big Ten athletic directors were socializing a 24-team concept dubbed "4-4-4-4-2-6"—four automatic bids for each Power Four conference, two for the Group of Five/Six, and six at-large selections. This massive expansion, similar to the FCS playoff format, would address ACC and Big 12 concerns about equal treatment while creating enormous television inventory. FOX Sports CEO Eric Shanks publicly supported this direction, stating "I'm very much in favor of expanding the CFP. I don't see any reason why the CFP can't be 24 teams."

The SEC's Stunning Pivot: From 4-4-2-2-1 to 5+11

Initially, many SEC athletic directors supported the Big Ten's 4-4-2-2-1 model. The format would guarantee the SEC four playoff spots annually, with potential for a fifth through at-large selection. It also created opportunities for lucrative play-in games and reduced dependence on a selection committee that SEC officials viewed with growing skepticism after the 2024 playoff selections included four Big Ten teams but only three SEC teams despite the SEC's perceived superior strength of schedule.

However, at the SEC's May 2025 spring meetings in Destin, Florida, the conference's powerful head coaches staged what amounted to a revolt against the 4-4-2-2-1 format. The 16 SEC coaches strongly preferred a different approach: a "5+11" model with five automatic bids for conference champions and 11 at-large selections—essentially an expanded version of the current 12-team format's philosophy.

The coaches' objections were practical and strategic. Brian Kelly, former LSU Head Coach, articulated the core concern: "Take the top 12-14-16 teams. That's my personal opinion. We'd much rather have a situation where more of our teams could get in than limiting it with AQs." The coaches worried that teams participating in play-in games and advancing to the national championship would play 18 total games, creating player safety and academic concerns. More fundamentally, data presented at the meetings showed the SEC might actually place more teams in a 5+11 format than in a 4-4-2-2-1 format, since SEC depth would dominate at-large selections.

Georgia's Kirby Smart and Ole Miss's Lane Kiffin (now at LSU) became vocal advocates for reforming the selection committee's criteria rather than creating automatic qualifier systems. Smart noted that "there's no outcry saying it's unfair when the SEC gets 13 of 16 teams in the (NCAA) basketball tournament by using RPI," arguing that a metrics-based approach to selecting the best 16 teams would favor the SEC more than guaranteed spots. Kiffin went further, suggesting the playoff should "take the 16 best teams" and eliminate automatic bids altogether.

SEC Commissioner Greg Sankey listened to his coaches and publicly acknowledged the shift. "They talked about—I'll call it a 5+11 model—and our own ability to earn those berths," Sankey said at the conclusion of the spring meetings. "At the coaching level, the question is, why wouldn't that be fine? Why wouldn't we do that?" By the end of May 2025, the SEC appeared to be moving away from the 4-4-2-2-1 format it had previously supported, creating a potential rift with the Big Ten just months after their alliance was formed.

The Core Philosophical Divide

The fundamental disagreement between the Big Ten and SEC after May 2025 reflected incompatible philosophies about what a playoff should accomplish. The Big Ten wanted structure and predictability through automatic qualifiers, believing this would "take away power from a perceived inconsistent selection committee process and allow for better non-conference scheduling," according to sources familiar with the discussions. Conferences would control their own destinies through conference standings rather than depending on committee judgments.

The SEC, influenced heavily by its coaches, wanted the opposite: maximum flexibility for the selection committee to choose what they believed were genuinely the best teams, even if that meant taking five or six SEC teams and leaving out conference champions from weaker leagues. The SEC viewed automatic bids as welfare for inferior conferences, guaranteeing spots to teams that had no realistic chance of winning a national championship while potentially excluding elite SEC teams that had simply cannibalized each other during the regular season.

These competing visions created irreconcilable problems. The Big Ten's 4-4-2-2-1 model explicitly rejected the SEC's premise that merit alone should determine playoff access. The SEC's preferred 5+11 model explicitly rejected the Big Ten's desire to minimize committee influence. Neither conference was willing to subordinate its core principles to reach agreement, and neither needed to—both held veto power over any format change through the 2024 memorandum of understanding.

The Alliance Fractures

The SEC's May 2025 pivot to supporting the 5+11 format caught Big Ten officials completely off guard and effectively ended any pretense of SEC-Big Ten unity on playoff matters. According to Yahoo Sports, the Big Ten had "voiced private displeasure over moving to 5+11 if the SEC sticks to eight conference games," creating yet another point of contention. The Big Ten wanted the SEC to expand to nine conference games if they were going to embrace at-large heavy formats, believing eight-game SEC schedules provided an unfair advantage in win-loss records compared to nine-game Big Ten schedules. In August 2025, the SEC has officially implemented a nine-game conference football schedule beginning in 2026.

More fundamentally, the SEC's reversal revealed that the alliance had always been tactical rather than strategic. When it served both conferences' interests to present a united front against smaller conferences, they cooperated. But when their interests diverged on the actual structure of playoff expansion, cooperation evaporated immediately. The SEC prioritized its coaches' preferences and its own data analysis over maintaining Big Ten partnership, while the Big Ten refused to compromise on automatic qualifier structures that the SEC now opposed.

The Remaining Conferences' Dilemma

While the SEC and Big Ten battled over competing visions, the remaining conferences found themselves in an impossible position. The ACC and Big 12, represented by commissioners Jim Phillips and Brett Yormark respectively, became vocal opponents of the 4-4-2-2-1 format. "No one likes it," one ACC athletic director told Yahoo Sports after being briefed on the proposal at ACC spring meetings. ACC coaches recommended that their conference push for at least three automatic qualifiers rather than accept second-class status with only two.

Ironically, the ACC and Big 12 found themselves supporting the same 5+11 format that SEC coaches preferred, though for different reasons. The smaller power conferences saw 5+11 as preserving their access through guaranteed conference champion bids while relying on at-large selections to reward strong teams regardless of conference affiliation. Big 12 Commissioner Brett Yormark told The Athletic he welcomed the formula "because we've got to earn it on the field. I tell my room, you're rewarded when you perform at the highest levels." Utah athletic director Mark Harlan echoed this sentiment: "I think the public and we agree that you have to win this thing on the field…Then Big 12, let's win more games. Let's get after it."

However, the alignment between the SEC coaches and the ACC/Big 12 on 5+11 didn't represent true alliance so much as parallel interests. The SEC believed it would dominate at-large selections under any committee-based model, potentially placing five or six teams. The ACC and Big 12 simply wanted to preserve their conference champions' automatic access. Both could support 5+11, but they supported it for entirely different strategic reasons—reasons that might diverge dramatically once the selection committee actually started making decisions under the new format.

The non-power FBS conferences (American, Mountain West, MAC, CUSA, Sun Belt) and the rebuilt Pac-12 had even less leverage. Any format being seriously discussed guaranteed them at most one or two collective automatic bids, meaning at least four of the six conferences would have their champions excluded annually. These conferences couldn't effectively advocate for their interests because they lacked the economic power to threaten the negotiations, and they couldn't form a unified coalition because their interests were fundamentally competitive—each wanted to be the Group of Five conference that received the automatic bid.

Continued Stalemate

The CFP set a December 1, 2025 deadline to inform ESPN of any format changes for the 2026 season. The deadline was extended to January 23, 2026. As that deadline approached, the impasse only deepened. Mississippi State president and CFP Board of Managers chair Mark Keenum told the SEC Network in late November: "We have to make a decision before the end of this month if we're going to expand to 16 next year. I'll be honest, I'm not very optimistic that we'll get to that, but we'll keep working on it."

The obstacles to agreement proved insurmountable in the available timeframe. The Big Ten insisted on automatic qualifier structures that the SEC coaches had rejected. The SEC wanted committee process reforms that the Big Ten believed would enable bias and politicking. The ACC and Big 12 refused to accept formats that gave them fewer guaranteed spots than the mega-conferences. The non-power FBS Conferences threatened legal action if excluded entirely. Notre Dame demanded protection for its independent status. No proposal could satisfy even half of these competing demands simultaneously.

The deadline passed with no agreement. The 12-team playoff format would remain in place for 2026, not because anyone believed it was adequate, but because the alternative was chaos. The SEC and Big Ten, who controlled the format through their memorandum of understanding, had achieved perfect gridlock—each could block the other's preferred solution, but neither could advance their own. The alliance that was supposed to consolidate their power had instead paralyzed college football's postseason.

Why My 32-Team Playoff Solution Works Best

The 32-team playoff format solves the zero-sum dynamics that have plagued every previous system and ended negotiations in stalemate. Rather than forcing conferences to fight over a limited number of spots, it creates enough room for everyone while still rewarding competitive excellence. Most importantly, it addresses the core concerns of both mega-conferences while preserving access for all of FBS.

Eliminating Zero-Sum Competition

The fundamental problem with 4-team and 12-team playoffs, and even the proposed 16-team formats, is that they create artificial scarcity. When only 12 or 16 teams can participate, every spot given to one conference is a spot denied to another. This makes cooperation impossible because conferences are negotiating over a fixed pie where one side's gain is necessarily another side's loss.

A 32-team playoff eliminates this dynamic entirely. With 10 automatic bids for all conference champions and 22 at-large selections, there is room for the SEC to place 6-8 teams, the Big Ten to place 6-8 teams, AND every conference champion to receive an automatic bid. The SEC gets rewarded for their depth. The Big Ten gets rewarded for their strength. Smaller conferences get their champions in. Everyone wins.

This abundance of spots doesn't mean the playoff lacks competitiveness. The seeding structure ensures that regular-season excellence matters enormously. The top 16 seeds receive home games in the first round, and the top 8 seeds are guaranteed home games through the second round if they win. A team that goes 12-0 in the SEC isn't treated the same as a team that goes 10-2 in the Sun Belt, even though both make the playoff. Performance determines seeding, and seeding determines path to the championship.

Addressing SEC Concerns About Depth and Merit

The SEC's primary objection to previous proposals has been that they don't adequately reward conference depth and strength of schedule. A 32-team playoff addresses this directly by providing enough at-large bids to accommodate deserving teams from strong conferences. If the SEC has 7 teams ranked in the top 25, all seven can make the playoff. If the Big Ten has 6 teams that deserve consideration, all six can earn bids.

The seeding structure also ensures that SEC excellence is rewarded tangibly. An undefeated SEC team will almost certainly earn a top-4 seed, guaranteeing two home games and an easier path through the bracket. An 11-1 SEC team that dominated its schedule will likely earn a top-16 seed with at least one home game. Even a 9-3 SEC team that lost to three ranked opponents might earn an at-large bid as an unseeded team or have a slim chance at a top 16 seed, giving them a chance to prove themselves in the playoff.

The revenue distribution model addresses SEC financial concerns by scaling payouts with advancement. First-round participation earns $7M, but making the national championship game earns a cumulative $60M for conference members. This creates enormous financial incentives for regular-season excellence because better seeding leads to easier paths, more wins, and exponentially higher revenue. The SEC's depth would translate directly to conference wealth as multiple teams advance deep into the playoff.

Most importantly, the 32-team format lets the SEC prove its claims about competitive superiority on the field. If the SEC truly believes their 5th-best team would beat most conference champions, they can demonstrate it in playoff games rather than argue about it in committee meetings. Head-to-head competition settles debates that ranking systems never can.

Addressing Big Ten Concerns About Access and Tradition

The Big Ten's emphasis on guaranteed access for conference champions is fully accommodated in a 32-team format. All 10 FBS conference champions receive automatic bids, ensuring that winning your conference always means something. This preserves the importance of the regular season and conference races while still allowing at-large bids for deserving teams that don't win their conference.

The format also preserves and enhances traditional bowl games by using the New Year's Six bowls for quarterfinal and semifinal games. The Rose Bowl can maintain its New Year's Day prominence by hosting a semifinal game annually, preserving its tradition while integrating it into the playoff structure. The Sugar, Orange, Cotton, Peach, and Fiesta Bowls rotate hosting duties for the remaining quarterfinal and semifinal games, ensuring all maintain relevance and revenue.

Geographic concerns about travel are addressed through the campus site structure for early rounds. The first two rounds occur at the higher seeds' home stadiums. This minimizes travel as best as possible and creates incredible home-field atmospheres that enhance the playoff experience. Only the final 8 teams face neutral-site bowl games, at a point in the season when extended travel is more manageable.

The Big Ten's concerns about competitive balance and long-term sustainability are addressed by ensuring all conferences maintain pathways to the playoff. This prevents the concentration of talent and resources into just two mega-conferences, which would eventually make the sport less interesting even for SEC and Big Ten fans. Upsets and Cinderella stories keep college football compelling, and a 32-team format creates more opportunities for them.

Preserving Competitive Balance Across FBS

The automatic bid structure for all 10 conference champions ensures that every FBS conference maintains a stake in the playoff structure. This prevents the catastrophic scenario where the SEC and Big Ten break away from the rest of FBS, fragmenting the sport and destroying decades of tradition and regional rivalries. As long as every conference has a pathway to the national championship, there's a reason to maintain FBS unity.

This structure also preserves recruiting balance to some degree. High school players who want to compete for championships can look at schools across all conferences, knowing that an undefeated season at Boise State or Memphis provides a path to the playoff just as surely as a season at Georgia or Michigan. While the SEC and Big Ten will always have recruiting advantages, a 32-team playoff prevents them from monopolizing championship opportunities entirely.

The seeding structure maintains appropriate competitive hierarchy while allowing for upsets. A 12-0 Mountain West champion might make the playoff as a top 16 seed. This would allow them to host at least one playoff game against an unseeded team. While there are those that don't like Cinderella stories to happen in College Football, we already had a Cinderella story in 2021. American Conference Champion Cincinnati made the 4-team playoff in 2021, becoming the first non-power FBS conference champion to make the playoffs.

Economic Incentive Alignment

The revenue model creates powerful incentives that align everyone's interests toward making the playoff succeed. Conferences earn money based on their teams' advancement, incentivizing them to schedule challenging non-conference games that prepare teams for playoff competition. Schools earn hosting revenue from first and second-round home games, making regular-season excellence directly profitable. Bowl games maintain relevance by hosting quarterfinals and semifinals, ensuring their economic survival.

The scale of potential revenue also makes the format economically compelling for everyone. The total playoff distribution could exceed $550M annually, dwarfing the current CFP payouts. Television networks would pay premium rates for 31 playoff games spanning five rounds, including multiple games in prime holiday time slots. Ticket sales from campus-site games would directly benefit host schools rather than neutral-site bowls, creating new revenue streams for successful programs.

Most importantly, the revenue structure discourages breakaway scenarios. If the SEC and Big Ten tried to form their own playoff system excluding other conferences, they'd be walking away from hundreds of millions in potential revenue that comes from the excitement of cross-conference matchups and underdog stories. A 32-team playoff makes staying together more profitable than breaking apart, which is the only sustainable long-term solution.

Ending the Impasse Through Abundance

The negotiations have failed repeatedly because every proposal forces someone to lose. The SEC's preferred 5+11 format with minimal automatic bids leaves conference champions vulnerable to committee decisions. The Big Ten's 4-4-2-2-1 format with tiered automatic qualifiers creates a permanent hierarchy that smaller conferences can never escape. Every compromise proposal splits the difference but satisfies no one because the fundamental problem is scarcity.

A 32-team playoff doesn't require anyone to compromise their core values. The SEC doesn't have to accept weak conference champions taking spots from strong at-large teams because there are enough spots for both. The Big Ten doesn't have to sacrifice automatic access for conference champions because all champions get in. Smaller conferences don't have to accept second-class status because their champions receive the same automatic bid as SEC and Big Ten champions, even if seeding reflects strength of schedule differences.

This abundance creates a pathway to agreement that has eluded every other format. Conference commissioners can return to their schools and honestly say "we got what we needed." The SEC can claim victory by securing enough at-large bids to reward their depth—potentially placing 6-7 teams in a given year. The Big Ten can claim victory by preserving automatic access for all conference champions and maintaining bowl traditions. The ACC and Big 12 can claim victory by ensuring their champions always have a path. Smaller conferences can claim victory by maintaining their pathway to the national championship. Everyone wins because the pie is big enough for everyone to get an adequate slice.

Practical Implementation Advantages

The 32-team format also offers practical advantages that smaller playoffs can't match. Scheduling 31 games across five rounds is logistically complex but manageable, especially with the first two rounds on campus sites requiring minimal coordination. The quarterfinal and semifinal bowl games already have the infrastructure to host playoff games. The national championship game can use the same neutral-site rotation as the current system.

Academic coordination during December playoffs has proven precedent across all NCAA football divisions. Division I FCS has successfully managed a 24-team playoff field that runs through Thanksgiving Weekend and into January, with December postseason games occurring during finals week. Division II (32 teams since 2025) and Division III (40 teams) also navigate December playoffs alongside academic schedules. As the level directly below FBS, FCS provides the most relevant precedent: demonstrating that with proper coordination between universities, professors, and student-athletes, December playoff games are entirely manageable alongside academic commitments.

The key accommodations that make this work include:

  • Exam rescheduling coordination: Student-athletes work with the registrar's office and professors to take finals before travel or during alternative time slots
  • Flexible practice schedules: Morning or evening practices allow dedicated study time during finals week
  • Institutional support: Registrar offices and professors accommodate exam scheduling conflicts for student-athletes participating in playoff games
  • Limited disruption window: Only teams advancing deep in the playoffs miss significant class time in January—most teams complete their seasons during winter break

For detailed coordination protocols, see Off the Field considerations. The FCS model proves this system works - FBS simply needs to adopt similar policies at scale.

The format also builds naturally on the 12-team playoff infrastructure that already exists. The selection committee already ranks the top 25 teams; expanding to rank the top 32 is incremental. The technology for broadcasting and managing the playoff is already proven. The financial structures for distributing revenue are already established. We're not building from scratch; we're scaling up an existing successful model.

The Only Sustainable Path Forward

Ultimately, the 32-team playoff works because it's the only format that gives everyone enough of what they need to live with what they don't get. The SEC and Big Ten get to place multiple teams and earn massive revenue. Smaller conferences get guaranteed access and respect. Traditional bowl games survive and thrive. Media partners get premium inventory. Fans get more meaningful games and clearer championship pathways.

Compare this to the alternatives. Maintaining the 12-team playoff leaves everyone frustrated with artificial cutoffs and controversial selections. The Big Ten's 4-4-2-2-1 proposal creates a permanent caste system that would accelerate conference realignment chaos as schools desperately try to join the SEC or Big Ten. The SEC's 5+11 proposal leaves too much power with a selection committee that both conferences have criticized as inconsistent and biased. A 24-team playoff addresses some access issues but uses the awkward 4-4-4-4-2-6 structure that still creates tiers among conferences and doesn't fully resolve the zero-sum competition. While the ACC and Big 12 would get the same automatic bids as the SEC and Big Ten, factoring in the at-large bids, they, along with the non-power FBS conferences would still get fewer guaranteed spots than the SEC and Big Ten.

Most ominously, the current stalemate raises the specter of an SEC-Big Ten breakaway. If these two conferences can't agree on a format with the other eight FBS conferences, they might eventually conclude that breaking away is simpler than negotiating. Such a breakaway would be catastrophic for college football—destroying traditional rivalries, fragmenting the sport's fanbase, creating antitrust issues, and transforming college football into a closed professional league that abandons everything that makes it unique.

The 32-team playoff prevents this nightmare scenario by making unity more valuable than separation. The format is large enough to accommodate everyone's legitimate interests, structured enough to maintain competitive integrity, and lucrative enough to discourage breakaway schemes. It respects the SEC and Big Ten's dominance while preserving pathways for every conference. It balances tradition with innovation, access with merit, and stability with competitiveness.

The crisis hovering over college football isn't really about playoff formats or automatic bids. It's about whether the sport can maintain its unity in the face of massive power imbalances. Can college football survive as one sport when two conferences control most of the money, talent, and influence? Can we create a system that acknowledges this reality while preventing it from destroying everything else?

The 32-team playoff answers yes to both questions. It provides a framework for unity that acknowledges power imbalances while preventing them from fracturing the sport. It creates enough room for the powerful to dominate without forcing everyone else into irrelevance. It's not a perfect solution for anyone, but it's a good enough solution for everyone—which is the only type of solution that can actually be implemented.

The SEC and Big Ten control college football's future through their 2024 memorandum of understanding. They can continue deadlocking each other over competing visions that satisfy neither, watch college football spiral into chaos and potentially litigation, or they can recognize that the 32-team playoff offers both conferences everything they actually need while preserving the sport they claim to value.

The choice is theirs. The 32-team playoff represents the only sustainable path forward. It is the only format that can do the following:

  • End the Impasse
  • Preserve FBS Unity
  • Reward Excellence
  • Provide Access
  • Generate Revenue
  • Keep College Football Recognizable as the Sport We Love

It's not just the best solution. It's the only solution that can actually work.
