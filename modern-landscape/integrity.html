Importance of Integrity

The 32-team playoff built on merit-based selection is only as credible as the games used to evaluate that merit. Every resume, ranking, and decision in this proposal, and in any playoff selection process, assumes that teams are authentically competing to win every game on their schedule. Strength of schedule, quality wins, and bad losses all carry meaning only if the underlying results reflect genuine competitive effort. If that assumption breaks down, the entire selection process loses its foundation.

Gambling and Competitive Integrity

Gambling in college sports has become a major concern. The NCAA has a rule that prohibits gambling on all forms of sports. Any athlete who bets on their own team or their own athletic department faces permanent ineligibility. This rule exists for a simple reason: the moment a player has a financial stake tied to the outcome of a game they're playing in, the integrity of that game is compromised, regardless of whether the outcome was actually influenced. The rule is about removing the incentive structure that makes wrongdoing possible in the first place.

However, in 2026, Texas Tech transfer, Brendan Sorsby, was granted a temporary injunction by a Texas court to allow him to play in 2026 anyway, a ruling the NCAA has appealed. He was ruled permanently ineligible by the NCAA after admitting to placing thousands of bets over four years. Sorsby admitted to wagering at least $90k in college football games. The wagers included Indiana football games while he was a player there, some of which were against his own team. Since he well over exceeded the $800 threshold on bets, he should have been permanently ineligible to play (rather than serving a 2-game suspension). The Texas court ruling faced backlash among college football fans and university administrators, with all Big 12 teams threatening to not play Texas Tech as well as other schools strongly considering not scheduling future games against them. Later in June 2026, the Big 12 filed a lawsuit against Texas Tech. Hours later, Sorsby declared for the NFL supplemental draft to end the eligibility saga.

Gambling addiction is a legitimate health issue. Any athlete struggling with it deserves support, treatment, and compassion. However, there is an important distinction between addiction as a reason for compassion and addiction as a justification for restoring eligibility. A program can fully support an athlete's recovery by covering treatment, providing mentorship, and ensuring long-term well-being, while still upholding the eligibility consequences the rules require. These two responses are not in conflict. Treating them as mutually exclusive, as the Sorsby ruling effectively does, risks setting a precedent where the severity of a violation becomes negotiable based on an athlete's legal resources and the sympathy of an individual court. What should have happened instead is the player should seek help for gambling addiction, but still be permanently ineligible considering the amount of money he placed on betting in college sports. If treating as an either or, the school's reputation will be tarnished, no matter how much the school defends the ruling by a judicial court.

Sign-Stealing and Competitive Integrity

In 2023, the NCAA opened an investigation into sign-stealing accusations against the Michigan football staff members. The scope included both video and paper trail. Connor Stalions purchased tickets for more than 30 games over 3 years at 11 different Big Ten schools. Stalions referred to his involved friends and family as the KGB, and the scheme planned to scout at least 13 future opponents over 52 contests. He referred to sideline footage obtained as "dirty film".

On November 3, 2023, Connor Stalions resigned from his position. However, the investigation continued. The investigation centered on the NCAA bylaw, which states the following:

Off-campus, in-person scouting of future opponents (in the same season) is prohibited, except as provided in Bylaws 11.6.1.1 and 11.6.1.2.

The NCAA alleged that Stalions sent friends and family to opponent's games to record future opponents' signs. The NCAA does ban teams from using electronic equipment to record opponents' signals during its own game. Bylaw 11.6.1.1 states that an institutional staff member cannot in-person advance scout a common opponent in the same season. In addition, Michigan did not cooperate with the NCAA investigation.

After nearly two years of investigating, the NCAA did find that Jim Harbaugh ran a program that largely neglected the rules compliance. Michigan received major financial sanctions along with other penalties. The program was stripped of postseason revenue in 2025-26 and 2026-27, was given a $50,000 fine plus 10% of the football program's budget, as well as a fine equivalent to the 10% cost of scholarships given at the end of the 2025–26 school year. Jim Harbaugh was given a 10-year show-cause for failing to monitor and lack of cooperation. Connor Stalions was given an 8-year show-cause for his violations. Michigan was also punished with four years of additional probation.

While some defended Michigan, others were not happy with what they did. Almost half of the coaches polled in the Athletic survey rated Stalions' actions as extremely serious, rating it a 5 out of 5. From the NCAA findings, the sign-stealing scandal broke many rules. The Michigan football team committed Level I violations as well as Level II violations. While the punishment does not correlate to the findings, it was serious where it has affected the outcome of games. The Big Ten leadership and the ones who enforced the sanctions did not do enough to appropriately enforce punishments. An unbiased approach would have looked like the following:

  • Either forfeit games, declare them as no-contest, or vacate wins from the 2021-2023 seasons
  • Vacating their Big Ten Championships from 2021-2023 as well as their National Championship in 2023
  • Multi-Year Postseason Ban
  • Enforce lifetime bans for those involved as well as the applied fines that were administered

Transfer Portal and Roster Integrity

Competitive integrity also ties to the transfer portal. The transfer portal's current structure, where players can move between programs with increasing frequency and minimal restriction, raises its own questions about roster stability and competitive balance, which this site addresses in more detail on its transfer portal page. My proposed transfer system, paired with clearer eligibility timelines, would help ensure that competitive balance reflects program development rather than season-to-season roster churn driven purely by NIL bidding.

The Requirement Going Forward

As access to the playoff expands, the financial stakes attached to every regular-season game increase. More teams in contention means more games matter, which means more games carry betting interest, fairness, transfer leverage, and NIL implications. That makes the integrity of those games more important, not less.

A playoff structure where every conference champion earns a spot, and where at-large selection rewards a full season's body of work, only means something if every game leading up to that selection was played honestly and competitively. The "everyone wins" philosophy behind this proposal depends on one quiet assumption that everyone is actually doing their best to win fair and square.
