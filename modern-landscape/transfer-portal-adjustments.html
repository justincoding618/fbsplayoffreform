Transfer Portal Adjustments

Let’s talk about the transfer portal. The purpose of the transfer portal is to allow players opportunities to go to a different school if their current school isn’t working out. After entering the transfer portal, they can seek opportunities. They can continue to seek opportunities as long as the player is still in the portal even after the transfer portal period expires. If they decide to withdraw their name from the portal, they cannot talk with other schools.

Introducing the Transfer Portal

The transfer portal was first introduced on October 15, 2018 to help student athletes in all sports manage their transfers between colleges. It has since evolved to allow players more freedom to switch schools without previous restrictions. When first introduced, the football transfer portal used to have two periods. The first transfer portal period happened in December, while the second happened in the Spring.

It Showed Cracks

The system showed cracks. In 2024-25, college football student athletes got a 20-day window to enter the portal in December and a 10-day window in April. Coaches have been very vocal about the negatives outweighing the positives of the spring portal window. The very few positives about the spring portal window is the following:

  • Some contenders were able to get the final missing pieces for their upcoming season during the April portal window. Sometimes those last few needs don't become obvious until a team goes through spring practice.
  • Many players were able to earn big paydays in the most recent spring window simply because teams were desperate and eager to spend.

The negatives were the following:

  • Coaches, general managers and NIL collectives got tired of players signing deals in December and then asking for more money in April.
  • With unlimited transfers, players have a ton of leverage in the spring.
  • It's difficult to replace starters who leave at the end of April because top players can always get offered more money by someone else.
  • Some coaches took advantage of the spring window to run off underperforming players and free up more scholarships.

In addition, players were able to enter the transfer portal before their teams play in the bowl games. In addition, when the 4-team playoff expanded to 12 teams, some players also entered the transfer portal before their respective teams played in the playoff. It also has opened up criticism over players who transfer every year, and questions about whether unlimited transfers hurt players more than it benefits them.

Recent Transfer Portal Modifications

In October 2025, the spring portal was eliminated, while the winter transfer portal was moved from December to January 2 - 16, a 15-day transfer window. The transfer window exception granted to student football players after a new head coaching change was also reformed. Initially, student football athletes had a 30-day window to enter the transfer portal after a head coaching change. That has been reduced to a 15-day window. There is also a 5-day transfer window for players on teams whose last postseason game is anytime on or after January 12.

Fixing the Transfer Portal

We do know is that college football is trending towards the NFL. In the NFL, the new calendar year does not start until March due to the Super Bowl concluding in February. The NFL holds free-agency and their draft during the first two months of the off-season. NFL Teams would begin holding official team activities (OTAs) in May. However, in college, Spring practices happen in late-February/early March. The free agency period would happen in January/February.

The Unlimited Transfer Challenge

Perhaps the most contentious issue with the current transfer portal is the elimination of transfer restrictions. Under the current rules, players can transfer an unlimited number of times without sitting out a year. While this provides player freedom, it has created significant problems:

  • Annual "free agency" undermines roster stability: Teams can lose entire position groups in a single transfer window
  • NIL leverage spirals: Players can use the threat of transferring to negotiate higher NIL deals annually
  • Competitive imbalance accelerates: Wealthy programs can simply buy proven talent from smaller schools rather than developing players
  • Academic concerns intensify: Students transferring multiple times struggle to complete degrees, particularly in majors with sequential requirements

Several potential solutions have been proposed, though each has drawbacks:

Option 1: Reinstate the One-Time Transfer Rule with Graduate Exception

Return to allowing one penalty-free transfer, with additional transfers requiring sitting out one year unless transferring as a graduate with eligibility remaining. This was the pre-2021 model.

Advantages: Provides player freedom while discouraging serial transferring; maintains roster stability; preserves development culture

Disadvantages: May face legal challenges on restraint of trade grounds; doesn't account for coaching changes or other legitimate reasons for multiple transfers

Option 2: Transfer Windows with Diminishing Eligibility

Allow unlimited transfers but reduce remaining eligibility by one year for each transfer beyond the first. For example, a player who transfers twice would have only three years of eligibility remaining instead of four.

Advantages: Creates natural deterrent against frivolous transfers; maintains player freedom; avoids legal challenges

Disadvantages: Punishes players for legitimate transfers (coaching changes, family emergencies); may be seen as coercive

Option 3: Institutional Caps on Transfer Intake

Limit how many transfers any single program can accept per year (e.g., maximum 10 incoming transfers annually). This doesn't restrict player movement but prevents teams from building entire rosters through the portal.

Advantages: Maintains player freedom; reduces "transfer portal recruiting wars"; encourages high school recruiting and development

Disadvantages: Could leave quality players without landing spots if all programs hit their caps; may disadvantage programs recovering from sanctions or coaching changes

Requiring At Least a Two-Year Commitment

The NCAA is considering adopting a mandatory two-year commitment for student athletes before they enter the transfer portal. Ohio State Head Coach, Ryan Day, is a strong supporter for the rule. He definitely provided a good reason for why he supports it, including mental health benefits and presevering through adversity. Here are some of his quotes from 11W on why he supports it:

“During that freshman season, typically, they’re not playing as much as they’d like. They’re going through challenges. They’re failing. But that’s part of being a freshman. You gotta fail. You gotta fail to learn. That’s how it works.” - Ryan Day, Ohio State Head Coach
"I think the mindset of saying, I’m somewhere for two years, I’m not gonna come up for a year and try to reassess this again. Plus, I just think it’s good for their overall mental health knowing that I’m somewhere for two years, and I know I have to push through adversity along the way and I got to grow. For a lot of us that went through that, we had four years. Transferring wasn’t an option. You had to figure it out. That’s a healthy thing." - Ryan Day, Ohio State Head Coach

While a two-year commitment is a good standard to do, we also have to consider the extreme cases:

  • What if a student athlete does not want to commit to a school for more than a year after graduating from high school or transferring?
  • What if a hardship happens during their two-year commitment?
  • Should a student athlete also commit two years to the school they transferred to as well?
  • What if a head coach is fired by their respective team or is hired by another team?

If we're able to also tackle the extreme cases, having a two-year commitment would definitely be a good implementation.

A Recommended Approach

You may be wondering what my recommended approach is to handle the transfer portal? What matters most for a 32-team playoff is that the transfer portal window occurs after the championship game, ensuring that:

  • Players on playoff teams aren't distracted during championship runs
  • Coaches can focus on their teams rather than recruiting during the playoffs
  • The transfer window doesn't interfere with playoff preparation and execution
  • All teams have equal access to transfer recruiting once the season concludes

Here is what I would recommend.

All Players Must Honor a 2-Year Commitment

In community and junior colleges, most students are there for two years. After two years, they can transfer their college credits and pursue a bachelor's degree at another school. Committing to a school for at least two years is the right approach for the following reasons:

  • It lets the players adapt to their environment during their first year.
  • It's ok to fail. If something goes wrong, you learn from your mistakes.
  • It's better to start over if things do not work out in two years.
  • The retention is important to be successful in one place.

After two years of staying at the same school, players would then be allowed to enter the transfer portal. However, after they commit to the school they transferred to, they also would have to honor a 2-year commitment for that transferred school before entering the transfer portal again.

What If Players Cannot Honor a 2-Year Commitment

If any player is not able to honor their 2-year commitment, then they must provide a legitimate reason for not honoring it. The reasons can be the following:

  • Head Coach was fired or left for another school
  • They are facing extreme hardships and cannot stay
  • They feel homesick and want to live closer to family

If any player cannot commit to a given school for at least two years due to any of the following reasons above, then they would be allowed to transfer without sitting out despite not honoring their 2-year commitment. If said players decide to enter the transfer portal without giving a legitimate reason of not honoring their 2-year commitment, they would have to sit out a year after only committing to their previous school for one year and transferring to their respective school.

Combining Transfers With Years of Eligibility

The NCAA is proposing new eligibility rules that would allow athletes five years to compete in college sports. I think this is the right approach. That is because students cannot compete as collegiate athletes forever.

Basically, with this in place, a student athlete would only be able to enter the transfer portal up to two times. This would also mean that if the student athlete decides to enter the transfer portal for the second time, they would only have one year of eligibility left. After that, their eligibility expires. For example, if a student athlete honors at least their two-year commitment at Alabama, they would be allowed to enter the transfer portal afterwards. Let's say they decide to transfer to USC. After transferring to USC, they would then have to honor a 2-year commitment at USC. After 2 years, the player can either stay at USC for one more year or enter the transfer portal one more time. However, if they enter the transfer portal for the second time, they only have one year of eligibility remaining, so they would have to choose wisely which school they want to play for their final year of eligibility.

What if a player stays at their school for at least the first three years? If a player stays at their school for the first three years, because the player would also have to honor a 2-year commitment at the school they transferred to, they would only be allowed to transfer once. Once they commit to a school that they want to play, they cannot enter the transfer portal again because they have to honor a 2-year commitment unless any of the stated reasons happen. For example, let's say a student athlete stays at Wisconsin for three years and decides to enter the transfer portal. They then decide to commit to Ohio State. Since they would need to honor a 2-year commitment, Ohio State would be their last stop for their collegiate athletic career. They would not be allowed to enter the transfer portal again unless their head coach was fired or left for another school, they face extreme hardships, or feel homesick and want to live closer to family. If a student athlete stays at their school for four years, the same would apply except they would only have one year of eligibility remaining.

What If a Player Decides to Enter the NFL Draft

If a player decides to declare for the NFL Draft, they would lose all their remaining eligibility. They would not be allowed to compete in collegiate athletics after entering the NFL Draft.

For all the other aspects of the Transfer Portal, the NCAA, conferences, and potentially Congress will need to determine the appropriate balance on transfer frequency limits. Whatever policy emerges, timing the portal after the championship resolves the immediate conflict between transfers and postseason competition.
