64-Team Playoff Field

A 64-team playoff field would likely involve remerging Division I FBS and Division I FCS into Division I football. The top Division I football teams clinch a playoff spot outright. All FBS and FCS conference champions (except for the MEAC and SWAC) would earn automatic bids to the 64-team playoff. The rest of the field would be filled with at-large bids. With the remerging of Division I FBS and FCS, the secondary and tertiary playoff systems would gain additional teams with winning records that they can select. The Celebration Bowl would continue to pit the MEAC and SWAC Champions. Overall, without expanding the secondary nor tertiary playoff fields nor adding a quaternary playoff field, a total of 114 teams out of 266 teams would participate in any meaningful postseason. However, the overall purpose is to dive deep into a scenario where a 64-team playoff could happen.

The Importance of Remerging Division I FBS and FCS

What happens if Division I FBS and FCS continue to exist? If a 64-team field happens in Division I FBS only, selecting 64 teams out of 138 teams is just a little under half of the entire Division I FBS pool at 46.3%. However, the problem really comes into play for postseason opportunities outside the 64-team field.

Looking at the breakdowns, the selection percentages become very high if a 64-team playoff is administered for only Division I FBS. The best possible cases is moving all the bowl games of the proposed secondary playoff system plus adding two more bowl games into the consolation pool of first and second round participants of the 64-team field (24 more bowl games would join the top ten bowl games into the 64-team field). Then, we can either leave the seven remaining bowl games as stand-alone games or reduce the secondary playoff field down to 16 teams to incorporate the seven remaining bowl games plus four wildcard consolation bowl games for the first round secondary playoff participants.

Overall, doing a 64-team playoff for Division I FBS is likely too much. Even though the thrid and fourth scenarios were the best possible cases, those scenarios involved eliminating the tertiary playoff system and either eliminating the secondary playoff system outright or reducing the secondary playoff system as a 16-team field to incoporate the seven leftover bowl games. In addition, there would be far too many at-large teams. With 10 conference champions earning automatic bids, a 64-team Division I FBS playoff field would have 54 at-large teams. Every Division I FBS conference would have the ability to send multiple teams into the playoff. Conference championships become meaningless when every Division I FBS conference is able to send multiple teams into the 64-team playoff field every year. It incorporates both the non-power FBS teams and power conference teams significantly too well.

The Main Solution

To incorporate the 64-team playoff field, Division I FBS and FCS would remerge together as Division I football. This will open opportunities for both Division I FBS and Division I FCS to play in the biggest stage and compete for a National Championship.

MEAC and SWAC

Since 2015, the MEAC and SWAC only send their conference champions to the Celebration Bowl. MEAC teams could become eligible at-large bids for additional postseason opportunities if they do not win the conference championship. The SWAC, on the other hand, do not send any team to the postseason other than the Celebration Bowl. This is because teams in the SWAC end the regular season later than all the other Division I FCS teams. If the MEAC and SWAC continue to only send their conference champions to the Celebration Bowl, a remerged Division I FBS and FCS as Division I football would likely lead both conferences becoming zero-bid leagues to the 64-team playoff. This is because in the remerged Division I FBS and FCS, power conferences would have an excellent chance of sending multiple teams, while a few non-power FBS conferences and few FCS conferences would have a chance to send multiple teams to the 64-team playoff. The rest of the conferences of the remerged Division I FBS and FCS would become one-bid leagues in most years due to their weaker strength than other conferences.

Playoff Composition and Bracket Structure

The playoff composition and bracket structure for the 64-team playoff field would work as follows:

  • 21 Automatic Bids for Conference Champions + 43 At-Large Bids
  • The top 32 teams, nationally seeded from 1-32, plus the 32 unseeded teams would compose of the 64-team playoff field
  • Each regional would have four national seeds and four unseeded teams

Division I Women's Volleyball has seeded the top 32 teams since 2022. However, they only used seeds 1-8 in each regional. Instead of seeding the top 32 teams from 1-8 in four 16-team regionals, the 64-team playoff can become first playoff to nationally seed teams from 1-32.

A team becoming a top 8 seed would host three playoff games. A team seeded 9-16 would host at least two playoff games. They would be able to host the regional final game if at least one of the top 8 seeds is eliminated in the first or second round. A team seeded 17-32 would host at least one playoff game. They would be able to host a second round game and/or the regional final game if at least one of the top 16 seeds is eliminated in the first or second round. If an unseeded team defeats their seeded opponent and faces an unseeded team in the second round and/or regional finals, home field would go to the team with the highest ranking.

After the first three rounds, the final three rounds would still happen at neutral sites. The New Year’s Six Bowl Games would continue to host the quarterfinal and semifinal rounds, with the Rose Bowl continuing as an annual semifinal game and the National Championship game continuing to happen at a pre-determined neutral site.

Scheduling Strategies

The first round schedule would have 16 games on Friday after Thanksgiving and 16 games on Saturday after Thanksgiving. For two of the eight regionals, they can either do the following:

  • Kickoff their first round games on Friday after Thanksgiving and then kickoff their second round games on Wednesday evening/night after Thanksgiving. For example, if all teams in Regionals 4 and 8 play on Friday after Thanksgiving, the Regional 4 and 8 first round winners would have their next game on Wednesday. The first round winners would have 5 days between games. The second round Regional 4 and 8 winners would have the regional finals on the following Wednesday evening/night.
  • Kickoff their first round games on Friday after Thanksgiving and then kickoff their second round games on Thursday evening/night after Thanksgiving. For example, if all teams in Regionals 5 and 7 play on Friday after Thanksgiving, the Regional 5 and 7 first round winners would have their next games on Thursday. The first round winners would have 6 days between games. The second round Regional 5 and 7 winners would have the regional finals on the following Wednesday evening/night.

The other Friday first round winners would play on the following Friday, and all Saturday first round winners would play on the first Saturday of December. The rest of the main 32-team playoff schedule would still apply.

Upsides and Downsides

There are upsides and downsides to expanding to a 64-team playoff field and merging Division I FBS and FCS together.

Upsides

If Division I FBS and FCS merge together, expanding to a 64-team playoff could work for the following reasons:

  • Every Division I Conference Champion gets access to the playoff
  • 43 At-Large Bids is enough to ensure deserving teams aren't excluded
  • Secondary and Tertiary Playoff Systems still have a pool of teams with winning records to select
  • Celebration Bowl is kept to respect traditions to MEAC and SWAC members
  • Additional Postseason Inventory
  • Potential Revenue Increase

The reason expansion is always in discussion is to always provide more playoff spots for teams to contend for a National Championship. In addition, it provides more revenue with an expanded format.

The Downsides

Although expanding to a 64-team playoff would be a very cool addition, we also have the consider the downsides. Basically, this system is a complete longshot due to the following:

  • Football funding between Division I FBS and Division I FCS
  • Number of sponsored sports between Division I FBS and FCS
  • Reluctance to being open to merging Division I FBS and FCS together

The reason some may not be open to merging Division I FBS and FCS together comes down to the commitment of athletic funding and how many varsity sports each member must sponsor. Not all universities invest heavily into athletics. Division I FBS requires all members to at least sponsor 16 varsity sports, while Division I FCS requires all members to at least sponsor 14 varsity sports. For the merger between Division I FBS and Division I FCS to happen, the minimum for FBS would have to match with FCS.

Conclusion

Expanding the playoff field to 64 teams sounds like a good idea on paper. In any expansion scenario, expanding the playoff field would provide more postseason inventory for media networks. In addition, more revenue and postseason opportunities can also help the remerged Division I football continue to grow as a premier college football division. More teams in Division I means more opportunities to select the most deserving teams to contend for a National Championship.

However, sometimes expanding to a 64-team playoff field may not be a good idea. In short, it's a complete longshot. Football funding is different in all Division I FBS and Division I FCS members. The required number of sponsored sports is also different in Division I FBS and Division I FCS. While merging Division I FBS and FCS would reunite them in Division I football, power conferences would likely be reluctant to allow it to happen. However, this could also benefit them as well as 43 at-large bids would allow them to put more teams into the playoff field along with their conference champions.

Despite the downsides, remerging Division I FBS and FCS to Division I football and administering a 64-team playoff field may be the right answer. Maybe the potential bonuses and increased inventory and revenue that comes with a remerged Division I football and a 64-team playoff field can overcome the downsides. In addition, the secondary and tertiary playoff systems would still have a pool of teams with a winning record to select after the 64-team playoff field is determined. Once again, the upsides can definitely overcome the downsides to remerging Division I FBS and FCS as Division I football and evolving the playoff field to 64 teams.
