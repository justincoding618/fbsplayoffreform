Potential Expansion

What if Division I FBS wants to expand to a sixth round? Division III football was the first NCAA Division to implement a 6th round in their postseason playoff field. Whether the playoff field is the ideal size or not, there will always be discussions to potentially expand the playoff field.

Cases Against Expanding

While larger fields are theoretically possible, they create significant scheduling and structural problems that make 32 teams the optimal stopping point for FBS:

Why Division III's Path Doesn't Apply to FBS

Division III successfully expanded from 32 to 40 teams (adding a 6th round) in 2024, but their situation was fundamentally different from FBS:

  • Division III's problem: 28 conferences with autobids left only 4 at-large spots in a 32-team field—a 7:1 autobid-to-at-large ratio. Teams were being systematically excluded despite strong records.
  • Division III's future: If they ever expanded to 64 teams, the ratio would be 28 autobids + 36 at-large (44% autobids, 56% at-large)—still a healthy balance given their conference structure. However, this expansion is currently not in NCAA's plans.
  • FBS's situation: 10 conferences with autobids in a 32-team field creates 22 at-large spots—a 1:2.2 ratio (31% autobids, 69% at-large). FBS already has plenty of at-large opportunities.

Division III has 28 conferences competing for limited at-large spots. Division III needed to expand their playoff field to provide adequate at-large selection. FBS has only 10 conferences, so expanding would add more at-large opportunities. While some would favor more at-large opportunities, it would dilute the value of conference championships by making at-large selection easier.

The Army-Navy Game Dilemma

Expanding beyond 32 teams requires either:

  • Moving Army-Navy to stand-alone season opener: This eliminates the traditional standalone finale that honors both academies. While this would allow the playoff to start Thanksgiving weekend (creating more scheduling room), it sacrifices a beloved tradition.
  • Moving Army-Navy as the stand-alone game on Friday Night before Thanksgiving during the regular season finale (with or without conference championship games): This would still keep the traditional stand-alone finale that honors both academies. However, other teams would not be allowed to schedule any games during that day.
  • Keeping Army-Navy after Thanksgiving: This forces the playoff's final four rounds to be similar to the current 12-team schedule (late December through mid-late January), which would not align with the academic calendars for all universities.

The main contingency plan as mentioned in additional adjustments is to make the Army-Navy game continue to be a stand-alone game, but played on Friday Night before Thanksgiving.

The Rose Bowl Dilemma

The Rose Bowl's New Year's Day tradition is deeply traditional for many. Expanding the playoff field beyond 32 teams would either:

  • Push semifinals past New Year's Day (breaking tradition), which would mean concluding the playoff sometime deep into January
  • Compress earlier rounds into impossible timeframes, creating very non-ideal rest periods between playoff games
  • Require starting the playoff during Thanksgiving week (which would mean that the main contingency plan for the Army-Navy game mentioned earlier comes into play)

Declining Quality in Secondary and Tertiary Playoff Systems

The more the main championship playoff expands, the lower the quality of teams in the secondary and tertiary playoff systems:

  • More power conference teams making the expanded playoff field means less best available power conference teams to select in secondary and tertiary playoff systems
  • More non-power FBS teams making in secondary and tertiary playoff systems that would have not made the main expanded playoff field
  • Teams with losing records (at least one game below 0.500) would be selected into the secondary and tertiary playoff systems

Why 32 Teams is the Sweet Spot

A 32-team field is the sweet spot for the following reasons:

  • Fits within existing schedule constraints (Army-Navy stays after Thanksgiving, Rose Bowl stays New Year's Day)
  • Healthy autobid-to-at-large ratio (maintains conference championship importance)
  • 23.2% playoff access (comparable to NCAA Division I norms of 18-28%)
  • Five-round bracket structure is mathematically clean and traditional
  • Solves FBS's actual problem (access + depth) without creating new problems (excessive at-large bids)

The bottom line: Division III expanded to 40 teams because they had too few at-large spots. FBS expanding beyond 32 would create too many at-large spots, which would further undermine the value of conference championships. 32 teams is the optimal size given FBS's specific structure and constraints.

What If Expansion Becomes Inevitable

If expansion becomes inevitable despite the downsides, there would definitely have to be adjustments in order to keep the proposed playoff schedule structure intact. Like the proposed 32-team playoff field, the conference championship games would be eliminated permanently (as mentioned in the proposed regular season page). Conference champions would be determined concluding the regular season. The simplest solution, as mentioned in additional adjustments, is to make the Army-Navy game continue to be a stand-alone game, but played on Friday Night before Thanksgiving. By making this adjustment, implementing a 6th round to start around Thanksgiving Weekend would then not be a problem. The Rose Bowl would stay as an annual semifinal game, and the National Championship game would not have to be pushed to later in January.

Expansion Plan Overview

Here are some of the potential expansion plans if we do go this route:

Format Teams Automatic Bids At-Large Bids Postseason Access
Current 12 Teams 5 7 8.7%
Proposed 32 Teams 10 22 23.2%

Expanded Ver. 2 39 Teams 10 29 28.2%
Expanded Ver. 3 40 Teams 10 30 29.0%
Expanded Ver. 4 * 64 Teams 21 43 24.1%
*Only applicable if FBS and FCS merge together as Division I Football

For the first five formats, we're still retaining separate Division I FBS and FCS. The percentages for the first five formats are based on how many Division I FBS teams are selected out of all current Division I FBS teams, which is 138 teams. When we get to the 64-team scenario, this is where Division I FBS and FCS would be merged. This is why the selection percentage would be less than the 38, 39, and 40-team scenarios. Instead of a selection percentage out of only Division I FBS teams, the selection percentage for the 64-team field would be based on current Division I FBS and FCS teams combined together.

Now that I have mentioned the possible expansion scenarios, here is an overview of how they would work:

Expansion to 38, 39, or 40 Teams

This is a scenario where Division I FBS and Division I FCS continue to be separate subdivisions. The reason these three fields are together is because the play-in structure for those three fields are very similar to each other.

Expansion to 64 Teams

This scenario is likely going to involve a major structural change in Division I college football. For a 64-team playoff to work, Division I FBS and Division I FCS would need to merge together as Division I football.
