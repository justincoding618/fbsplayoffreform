Why 32 Teams?

College football faces an existential crisis. The current 12-team playoff is already showing serious flaws after just two seasons. On January 23, 2026, the SEC and Big Ten failed to reach an agreement on expansion. The playoff remains at 12 teams. Power conferences are discussing breaking away. The sport is fracturing.

A 32-team playoff isn't just a better format—it's the comprehensive solution that preserves FBS unity while solving every major problem facing college football.

This page provides an overview of why 32 teams is the right answer. Each section below links to a detailed analysis of that particular argument.

The Case for 32 Teams

1. NCAA Historical Precedent

Every NCAA football division has independently arrived at the same conclusion: divisions need playoff fields that provide 16-20% access. FCS, Division II, and Division III all followed similar evolutionary paths, expanding their playoffs as membership grew and conferences multiplied.

Key Facts:

  • FCS (129 teams): 24-team playoff = 18.6% access
  • Division II (161 teams): 32-team playoff = 19.9% access
  • Division III (241 teams): 40-team playoff = 16.6% access
  • FBS (136 teams): 12-team playoff = 8.8% access ← Worst in NCAA football

The 8-team format lasted exactly ONE season in FCS before being recognized as inadequate. Division II spent 52 years experimenting before getting it right in 2025. Division III took 51 years to reach adequate access. FBS should learn from their experience.

2. SEC-Big Ten Impasse

The SEC and Big Ten are locked in a bitter dispute over playoff expansion. Despite forming an alliance in February 2024, they now disagree on virtually everything. The SEC and Big Ten failed to reach an agreement, so the College Football Playoff remains at 12 teams for at least another season.

This outcome proves exactly what this proposal has been warning about: partial solutions lead to dysfunction and gridlock. The two most powerful conferences in college football, given sole authority over playoff decisions, could not agree on a path forward. The sport remains stuck with a format that both conferences acknowledge is inadequate, because neither's preferred alternative solves the fundamental problems. This is what happens when you optimize for one constituency rather than the entire ecosystem.

The Competing Proposals:

  • SEC wants 16 teams: 5 power autobids + 1 non-power FBS autobid + 11 at-large (which non-power FBS conference gets the bid?)
  • Big Ten wants 24 teams: 4 autobids per power conference + 2 non-power FBS autobids + 6 at-large (creates bye problems)
  • Neither solves the fundamental problem

A 32-team playoff eliminates the zero-sum dynamics. The SEC gets sufficient at-large depth (22 spots). The Big Ten gets broad access. All 10 conferences get guaranteed autobids. No one needs to break away.

3. Why Other Formats Fail

Every alternative playoff format creates new problems while leaving old ones unsolved:

8 Teams: Too exclusive. History proves it's always a starting point, never a destination. Would exclude multiple conference champions.

12 Teams (Current): Already showing flaws—conference championships become redundant, bye teams struggle (1-7 record in quarterfinals), teams 13-16 have legitimate grievances, access shrinking as FBS grows.

16 Teams: Impossible math. Either exclude non-power FBS conferences (creating two-tier system) or give all 10 conferences autobids with only 6 at-large spots (insufficient for power conference depth).

24 Teams: Byes create massive competitive imbalance. The gap between seed 8 (bye) and seed 9 (no bye) becomes the new controversy. Bracket structure complications.

Big Ten's Multiple Autobids: Guaranteeing 4 bids per power conference undermines the concept of "conference champion" and removes meritocracy. Creates massive inequity (16 power autobids vs. 2 non-power FBS autobids).

4. The Autobid-to-At-Large Balance

Division III's experience provides a critical lesson: when automatic bids consume too much of the playoff field, the system breaks.

By 2023, Division III had a 7:1 ratio of autobids to at-large (28 autobids, 4 at-large). With 240+ teams competing for only 4 at-large spots, deserving teams were systematically excluded. They had to expand to 40 teams.

Comparison of Ratios:

  • Division III (before expansion): 7:1 ratio → Crisis
  • Division III (current): 2.3:1 ratio → Fixed
  • Division II (current): 1:1 ratio → Balanced
  • FBS 16-team format (all conferences): 1.67:1 ratio → Tight
  • FBS 32-team format: 1:2.2 ratio → Optimal

A 32-team FBS playoff would have the healthiest autobid-to-at-large ratio in NCAA football, providing both guaranteed access AND meritocratic selection.

5. Revenue Maximization

The 32-team playoff generates approximately $556 million in total payouts across five rounds, with performance-based distribution that rewards competitive success:

  • First Round (16 games): $224M distributed
  • Second Round (8 games): $144M distributed
  • Quarterfinals (4 games): $96M distributed
  • Semifinals (2 games): $56M distributed
  • National Championship (1 game): $36M distributed

A team reaching the national championship game earns $60M for their conference. Conferences with multiple teams making deep runs accumulate substantial revenue. This creates financial incentives for:

  • non-conference scheduling (improves at-large chances)
  • Competitive conference play (seeding determines home-field advantage)
  • Conference-wide competitive depth (more teams = more revenue)

6. Preserves Conference Championship Flexibility

One of the major flaws of the current 12-team format: conference championship games become redundant when both participants are already playoff-bound. In 2024 and 2025, the SEC and Big Ten champions and their respective runner-ups were making the playoff regardless of the outcome.

The 32-team proposal solves this by making conference championships optional:

  • For teams eliminating the conference championship game, the automatic bid goes to the regular season conference champion (tiebreakers applied as needed)
  • Conferences can choose whether to hold conference championship games based on their own needs
  • If held, the winner of the conference championship game would earn the automatic bid to the playoffs.
  • Some might keep them for TV revenue; other conferences might eliminate them

This respects conference autonomy while removing the structural problem of meaningless championship games. Similar to how some Division II and Division III conferences hold championship games while others don't.

7. Competitive Balance Through Seeding

The 32-team format rewards regular season performance without creating the massive imbalances that byes create:

How Seeding Works:

  • Top 16 teams are seeded 1-16
  • Teams 17-32 are unseeded (includes lower-ranked conference champions)
  • Seeds 1-8 host two home games (first and second round)
  • Seeds 9-16 host one home game (first round only)
  • Unseeded teams must win on the road

Why This Is Better Than Byes:

  • Everyone plays the same number of games to advance—no multiple week layoffs
  • Home-field advantage is meaningful but doesn't create rhythm/rust problems
  • The regular season matters tremendously (difference between seeds 1-8, 9-16, and unseeded (17-32) is massive)
  • No arbitrary cutoff between "bye teams" and "non-bye teams"

Current 12-team data shows bye teams struggling: 1-7 record in quarterfinals (excluding Indiana). The extended layoff may be causing issues.

8. Prevents Power Conference Breakaway

The SEC is "in preliminary stages of considering a conference-only governance model." This isn't hypothetical—fragmentation is a real threat.

Why would power conferences want to break away?

  • Feeling constrained by limited at-large opportunities in current format
  • Belief their competitive depth isn't adequately rewarded
  • Frustration with governance structure and inability to reach agreements
  • Desire for greater control over revenue distribution

How 32 teams removes the incentive:

  • 22 at-large bids means the SEC can realistically send 6-8 teams in best years
  • Big Ten can do the same—both conferences get what they want
  • Performance-based revenue rewards competitive depth
  • All conferences maintain guaranteed access through autobids
  • No structural reason to destroy the sport's unified framework

The 32-team playoff is the only format that simultaneously satisfies power conference demands AND maintains FBS unity.

9. The Regular Season Still Matters

A common objection: "Won't expanding to 32 teams make the regular season meaningless?"

The opposite is true. The regular season determines:

  • Conference championships: Winners get automatic bids
  • At-large selection: 22 competitive spots based on committee rankings
  • Seeding (1-16): Determines who gets home-field advantage
  • Top 8 vs. Top 16: Difference between two home games and one
  • Seeded vs. Unseeded: Massive difference between seed 16 (home game) and seed 17 (road game)

Every game matters because seeding determines your path. The difference between seed 5 (two home games) and seed 17 (unseeded, on the road) is enormous. Teams will fight for every ranking position.

Compare this to other sports: the NFL playoffs include 14 of 32 teams (43.75% access) and the regular season still matters intensely for seeding.

10. Bowl Games Are Preserved, Not Eliminated

A common concern: "Won't a 32-team playoff kill the bowl system?"

The opposite is true. The 32-team format actually strengthens the bowl ecosystem by giving bowls meaningful roles and providing postseason opportunities for teams outside the playoff.

How Bowl Games Fit Into the System:

  • New Year's Six Bowls Host Playoff Games: The Rose Bowl, Sugar Bowl, Orange Bowl, Cotton Bowl, Peach Bowl, and Fiesta Bowl host the quarterfinals and semifinals on a rotating basis, maintaining their prestige and tradition
  • Low-Tier Playoff Systems: Teams outside the main playoff can compete in tiered playoff systems for additional postseason recognition
  • Traditional Bowl Slots Remain: Teams that don't make the 32-team playoff nor the low-tier playoff system still have bowl opportunities
  • More Teams Get Postseason Experience: With 136 FBS teams, 64 participate in playoff systems (32 CFP + 16 NPI + 16 CPC), 16 more play in traditional bowls, meaning 80 total teams get postseason opportunities.

The Low-Tier Playoff Solutions:

For teams that don't make the 32-team CFP, the proposal includes additional postseason structures:

  • The National Playoff Invitational (NPI) would serve for teams not selected to the CFP. This playoff would have 7 bowl games hosting the final three rounds, followed by four consolation bowl games, totaling up to 11 bowl games.
  • The College Playoff Crown (CPC) would serve for teams not selected to the CFP nor NPI. Like the NPI, the playoff would have 7 bowl games hosting the final three rounds, followed by four consolation bowl games, totaling up to 11 bowl games.
  • Bowl partnerships that maintain traditional matchups and regional connections
  • Player development opportunities for programs building toward CFP competitiveness

Complete Postseason Structure:

  • 32 teams: College Football Playoff (national championship path)
  • 16 teams: National Playoff Invitational (NPI - second-tier playoff)
  • 16 teams: College Playoff Crown (CPC - third-tier playoff)
  • 16 teams: Traditional bowl games (8 bowls remaining)
  • Total: 80 of 136 teams (58.8%) receive postseason opportunities

This ensures that most bowl-eligible teams (6+ wins) have a postseason destination, whether in a playoff system or traditional bowl game.

This approach is similar to college basketball: The NCAA Tournament is the premier championship, but the NIT, CBC, and CBI provide additional opportunities. Nobody argues basketball's postseason has "too many" teams—the tiered system works.

The Bottom Line

College football stands at a crossroads. The current 12-team format is already failing. The SEC and Big Ten cannot agree on what comes next. The sport faces potential fragmentation.

We have 50+ years of NCAA precedent showing us what works:

  • FCS learned that 8 teams was inadequate after one season
  • Division II spent 52 years experimenting before landing on 32 teams in 2025
  • Division III took 51 years to reach adequate access, expanding to 40 teams in 2024
  • All divisions converged on 16-20% playoff access as the sustainable standard

FBS has 136 teams and 10 conferences. Division II (161 teams, 16 conferences) just proved that 32 teams works. The math isn't complicated: 10 autobids + 22 at-large = access, merit, revenue, and unity.

And the bowl system isn't destroyed—it's enhanced with meaningful roles for NY6 bowls in the playoff and continued opportunities for teams outside the top 32.

The real question isn't "Why 32 teams?"
The question is: "Why would the largest and most prominent division in college football provide worse playoff access (8.8%) than Division II (19.9%) and Division III (16.6%)?"
